   116в†’   116в†’   116в†’
   117в†’   117в†’   117в†’### 2.1 Tier Philosophy: The "Access Ladder"
   118в†’   118в†’   118в†’
   119в†’   119в†’   119в†’The most successful think tanks do not sell content вЂ” they sell access to a differentiated experience built atop freely available research. Brookings Institution, Carnegie Endowment, and the European Council on Foreign Relations (ECFR) all make their core research, policy briefs, and commentary fully accessible without registration or payment [^549^] [^554^] [^196^]. Chatham House follows the same principle: its research publications are free to all; membership fees pay for events, The World Today magazine, and the International Affairs journal вЂ” not for basic research access [^29^]. This "open by default" norm is not altruism; it is a deliberate funnel strategy. Free research builds authority, drives SEO, and generates the trust that converts readers into members. SRC Advisory's tier architecture must follow this same logic: differentiate through experience, speed, exclusivity, and direct access вЂ” never by withholding foundational analysis from the public.
   120в†’   120в†’   120в†’
   121в†’   121в†’   121в†’**Naming strategy matters more than most organizations assume.** Research on membership psychology consistently shows that tier names shape conversion behavior. "Basic" signals inadequacy and triggers what behavioral economists call "bottom-tier aversion" вЂ” members feel they are starting at a deficient level rather than joining a valued community. Value-based names like "Essential," "Professional," and "Executive" convert 15вЂ“30% better than generic labels because they communicate what the subscriber receives rather than where they rank in a hierarchy [^163^] [^413^]. The Atlantic uses "Digital в†’ Print & Digital в†’ Premium в†’ Premium Plus" вЂ” a progression that never devalues the entry point [^169^]. Academic research on optimal tier differentiation for content platforms finds that the ratio between adjacent tiers should exceed 1.5x вЂ” anything less and subscribers fail to perceive meaningful value separation, leading to "tier collapse" where most subscribers cluster at the entry level [^510^].    276в†’   276в†’   276в†’
   277в†’   277в†’   277в†’#### 3.2.1 Time as a Tier Differentiator
   278в†’   278в†’   278в†’
   279в†’   279в†’   279в†’Traditional paywalls face structural tension: lock content too tightly and sacrifice reach; open too widely and eliminate subscription incentive. The reverse timewall makes *time* the gate. Content flows downward: Executive receives immediate access, Professional after 24 hours, Essential after 72 hours, Free after two weeks. Every tier eventually receives the same content, but higher tiers get it during the decision window when intelligence has maximum value.
   280в†’   280в†’   280в†’
   281в†’   281в†’   281в†’Two proven implementations validate the model. BoiseDev gives paying members first newsletter access, then publishes 24 hours later вЂ” generating "tens of thousands of dollars in reader revenue without locking the public out of a single article" [^259^]. Mittmedia made content free for the first hour, then premium-only, achieving a 20% conversion increase [^259^]. Both confirm: subscribers pay for temporal advantage even when content eventually becomes free. The reverse timewall is simultaneously a scarcity mechanism (higher tiers get first access) and a reach mechanism (content eventually becomes free) вЂ” resolving the central tension of paywall design.
   282в†’   282в†’   282в†’
   283в†’   283в†’   283в†’For SRC, a breaking event triggers a flash alert for Executive within 4 hours [^653^]. After 24 hours, expanded context becomes available to Professional. This cascade transforms one analytical product into four tier experiences without four production workflows.
