## 4. Pricing Structure & Psychology

### 4.1 CHF Pricing Architecture

The price point is not merely a number — it is a signal of positioning, a filter for audience quality, and the single largest determinant of lifetime revenue per member. Pricing in Swiss francs is non-negotiable for SRC: Swiss consumers expect CHF-denominated pricing, and using EUR or USD introduces friction and perceived foreignness that reduces conversion [^749^] [^750^]. The NZZ prices exclusively in CHF for this reason [^800^]. Switzerland's consistently top-quartile GDP per capita means elevated price points are normalized and associated with quality rather than elitism [^755^].

SRC's monthly pricing — Free at CHF 0, Essential at CHF 29, Professional at CHF 79, Executive at CHF 149 — is calibrated against intelligence and media benchmarks. At CHF 29, Essential undercuts the NZZ Pro entry of CHF 45/month (annual rate) [^800^] while sitting above mass-market subscriptions, signaling professional-grade value. At CHF 79, Professional sits between RANE Network's ~$80/month individual rate [^274^] and the Financial Times Premium Digital at $75/month [^51^]. The Executive tier at CHF 149 anchors the individual range, creating the psychological conditions for Professional to emerge as the compromise choice explored in Section 4.2.

Annual pricing delivers a 17% discount framed as "2 months free" — CHF 290, CHF 790, and CHF 1,490 respectively. This framing converts better than "Save 17%" despite being mathematically identical [^760^]. The reason is loss aversion: consumers respond more strongly to receiving something extra ("2 months") than to percentage abstractions. Annual subscribers retain at 92% after 12 months versus 68% for monthly — a 24-point gap [^762^] — and generate 40-71% higher lifetime value despite the discount [^760^] [^762^]. Defaulting to annual on the pricing page increases annual adoption 2-3x [^760^]. SRC should target 50-60% of new paid subscriptions on annual plans [^762^], while keeping monthly available as it lifts initial conversion by approximately 50% [^762^].

|:---|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---|
| Free | 0 | 0 | — | — | Lead generation; no payment friction |

The Essential→Professional multiple of 2.7x exceeds the 1.88x average Good→Better ratio [^794^], but this is intentional: Essential is a limited entry point, and the jump reflects the substantial feature expansion to unlimited reports, full archive access, and exclusive newsletters. The Professional→Executive multiple of 1.9x exceeds the 50% minimum gap requirement. The total 5.1x spread reflects that Executive is not merely "more Professional" but a different product — one with direct analyst access, invitation-only events, and the annual summit that transforms membership from content consumption into advisory relationship.

### 4.2 Pricing Psychology & Tactics

#### 4.2.1 The Decoy Effect and Compromise Positioning

The Executive tier at CHF 149 makes Professional at CHF 79 appear affordable through the decoy effect — adding an asymmetrically dominated option to make the target tier look like exceptional value [^786^] [^787^]. Simonson's research (1989) shows the middle option in a three-tier set gains a 17.5% larger market share than alternatives, a phenomenon known as the compromise effect [^831^]. When an upscale third option is added, the middle option's share rises 7 percentage points to 57% [^831^]. Physical placement amplifies this: center-positioning the compromise option adds 20.4% to 41.7% choice share, magnifying to +53.3% under time pressure [^834^]. SRC's pricing page should present tiers left-to-right as Free → Essential → Professional → Executive, with Professional visually centered and marked "Most Popular" — a label that specifically outperforms "Recommended" [^817^]. Free reinforces this architecture from the opposite direction: by providing a genuinely useful no-cost option, it makes Essential at CHF 29 feel like a real commitment rather than the cheapest possible choice.

#### 4.2.2 Annual Billing Defaults and Framing

When annual is the default, 40-60% of customers choose it; when monthly is default, fewer than 20% select annual [^461^]. The optimal display shows the monthly equivalent on annual plans ("CHF 79/month, billed annually") rather than the total annual amount, because the lower number anchors perception while the annual commitment locks in retention [^817^]. Clear pricing communication increases conversions 20-30% [^749^]. SRC's pricing page should lead with annual plans, use "2 months free" throughout, and relegate monthly to a toggle — available for those who seek it, but not the path of least resistance.

#### 4.2.3 Student and Young Professional Discounts

Chatham House offers the gold-standard model: Full at £345/year, Under-30 at £220 (36% discount), Student at £190 (45% discount) [^29^]. Critically, Chatham House positions Under-30 *above* its Associate tier at £195 — the youth discount is not the cheapest option, protecting brand prestige while maintaining accessibility [^29^].

SRC mirrors this structure: CHF 190/year for Under-30 (36% discount) and CHF 160/year for verified students (45% discount), both mapping to Professional-tier benefits. This ensures young members experience the full product and develop habits around the tier SRC wants them to maintain post-graduation. Verification uses .edu email for students and ID-based age confirmation for Under-30. Annual billing is required for all discounted plans. No free tier is offered to students — keeping them in the paid ecosystem establishes price acceptance and reduces future upgrade friction.

#### 4.2.4 Trial Length and Risk Reversal

SRC's 10-day free trial sits in the optimal conversion zone. Seven-day trials convert at 24% median, 14-day at 19%, and 30-day at only 14% — shorter trials outperform 30-day by 71% [^758^]. The mechanism is urgency: shorter trials create focused evaluation pressure, while longer trials breed complacency [^763^] [^766^]. A randomized study of 337,724 users found only a 0.81-point difference between 7-day and 30-day conversion, confirming that trial length matters less than activation during the trial [^761^]. SRC should maintain its 10-day trial and focus on guiding users through 3-5 activation actions rather than extending duration. The trial should remain opt-in without credit card, as 80% of SaaS trials are already opt-in [^467^].

A 30-day money-back guarantee on all paid tiers drives 10-20% conversion increases [^764^], with one case study showing 26% lift from adding a simple guarantee [^767^]. Extended guarantees produce even more dramatic results: moving from 90-day to 1-year guarantee doubled conversion (3% to 6%) with only a 3-point refund rate increase because the majority of refunds still occur within the original window [^767^]. For SRC, the guarantee reduces perceived risk at the exact moment when a prospective member evaluates whether the analysis justifies the price, and drops cart abandonment by up to 15% [^764^].

These tactics operate multiplicatively. The decoy effect drives attention to Professional, the badge validates that choice socially, the annual default locks in highest-LTV billing, the trial reduces signup friction, and the guarantee removes buyer's remorse. Together they create a conversion architecture where Professional annual emerges as the self-evident choice for serious professionals — precisely the member profile SRC needs.

### 4.3 Corporate Membership Pricing

Individual memberships build the funnel and brand; corporate memberships drive revenue and embed SRC into institutional workflows. The two-track architecture — parallel individual and corporate paths with connected nomination mechanics — is employed by Chatham House, CFR, and the Conference Board [^29^] [^133^] [^41^]. Corporate subscriptions provide 5-50 individual login accounts (nominated members), transforming the sale from "content access for the team" into "seats at the table for your people."

SRC's corporate pricing uses a headcount-band model with three operational tiers plus an academic/government exception. The Team tier at CHF 3,900/year covers 5 nominated seats with a shared dashboard, basic admin controls, and standard briefing access — CHF 65 per seat monthly, below the individual Professional rate of CHF 79, creating clear volume incentive. The Department tier at CHF 9,900/year for 20 seats adds usage analytics, a private quarterly briefing, and API access modeled on The Economist Pro's content integration approach [^852^] — at CHF 41.25 per seat monthly, a 48% per-seat discount that makes expansion compelling.

The Enterprise tier at CHF 24,900/year for 50+ seats includes SAML/SSO integration (table stakes for enterprise procurement) [^836^] [^837^], a dedicated account manager, custom Teams and Slack integrations [^852^], and up to 12 bespoke research engagements annually — effectively bundling advisory services into membership. Designed for annual invoice via QR-bill or SEPA [^804^], this price sits below CFR Gold at $100,000/year [^133^] and Chatham House Partnership at GBP 50,000+ [^113^], positioning SRC as accessible premium while commanding serious procurement consideration.

The Academic/Government tier at CHF 2,900/year — a 26% discount from Team — acknowledges constrained public-sector budgets while keeping SRC competitive with CIS SecureSuite's $2,400/year entry for organizations up to 99 employees [^838^]. Multi-year contracts should offer 5% for 2-year and 10% for 3-year commitments following the CIS model [^838^].

The pricing architecture expands naturally within client organizations: an individual subscriber scales to Team for their direct reports, then to Department or Enterprise as usage proves ROI. SaaS benchmarks show that consistent quarterly business reviews maintain net retention 15-20 points higher than reactive approaches [^870^] [^874^]. The economics decisively favor SRC: one Enterprise client at CHF 24,900 generates revenue equivalent to approximately 32 individual Professional annual memberships, with lower support overhead, near-zero churn, and the compounding effect of institutional reliance that makes non-renewal a visible organizational decision rather than an individual's silent cancellation.
